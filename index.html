I'm a proud generalist who understands that the spire of specialization, albeit a requisite for cutting edge progress in nearly every field, can be limiting when it comes to recognizing larger patterns and making novel associations that could lead to breakthroughs in science and technology. Consider the following analogy: The internal combustion engine was originally designed and built for the sole purpose of pumping water from flooded mines.

Find me a success story, and I'll show you a team. A group of compentent and communicative individuals working together towards common goals are able to successfully tackle more complex problems, better manage larger systems, and more consistently produce good results. The merits of teamwork are myriad, and arguments against are illogical and absurd. Yes, there are geniuses in this world, but most of the systems and constructs we are surrounded by were imagined and built by the collective brain power and aggregate work of organized groups of people. I believe that every team member can contribute and lead within a respectful and supportive work environment, and that when people in a healthy culture are asked to come together and rise to the occasion, they generally do.

Knowledge is the key to problem solving, and experience is the crucible that selects stronger problem solving stategies over weaker ones. When a work culture encourages education, the rewards are many and are a benefit to the entire operation. When team members are empowered to think critically and take initiative, a host of probems can be identified earlier, appropriate attention and resources can be deployed more quickly, and issues can be addressed and dealt with in process.
